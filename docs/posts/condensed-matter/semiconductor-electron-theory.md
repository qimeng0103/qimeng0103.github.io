## Introduction

Semiconductors occupy a unique position in condensed matter physics, bridging the gap between insulating and metallic behavior. Unlike metals where the Fermi level lies within a partially filled band, semiconductors have a small band gap separating a filled valence band from an empty conduction band. This seemingly minor difference leads to dramatically different physical properties and enables the entire modern electronics industry.

This article provides a comprehensive treatment of semiconductor physics, emphasizing the statistical mechanics of carrier generation and the transport properties that make semiconductors technologically indispensable.

## Part I: Band Structure and Impurity Levels

### 1.1 The Fundamental Difference: Metals vs. Semiconductors

**The Band Picture**

In the single-electron band theory, the key distinction between materials arises from the position of the Fermi energy relative to the band structure:

- **Metals**: Fermi level lies within a band - partially filled states allow conduction
- **Insulators**: Fermi level lies in a large band gap (greater than 5 eV) - no thermal excitation across gap at room temperature  
- **Semiconductors**: Fermi level lies in a small gap (0.1-3 eV) - measurable thermal excitation at room temperature

**The Role of Temperature**

For a band gap of 1 eV at room temperature:

$$
\frac{E_g}{k_B T} = \frac{1 \text{ eV}}{0.025 \text{ eV}} \approx 40
$$

While this ratio is large, the Boltzmann factor gives a small but not negligible occupation when multiplied by the enormous density of states.

### 1.2 Intrinsic Semiconductors: Carrier Generation

**Thermal Excitation Across the Gap**

In a pure (intrinsic) semiconductor, charge carriers are generated by thermal excitation of electrons from the valence band to the conduction band. Each such excitation creates:
- One electron in the conduction band (negative charge)
- One hole in the valence band (positive charge)

This electron-hole pair is the fundamental excitation in semiconductor physics.

**The Chemical Potential in Intrinsic Semiconductors**

At T = 0, the valence band is completely filled and the conduction band is empty. The Fermi level is conventionally placed at the middle of the gap.

At finite temperature, we determine the Fermi level by the charge neutrality condition: electron concentration equals hole concentration.

### 1.3 Extrinsic Semiconductors: Doping

**The Concept of Doping**

The controlled introduction of impurities (doping) allows us to engineer the carrier concentration and type:

- **n-type**: Addition of donor impurities provides extra electrons
- **p-type**: Addition of acceptor impurities provides extra holes

**Hydrogenic Impurity Model**

Consider a donor atom with one extra valence electron compared to the host. This extra electron is weakly bound to the positively charged donor ion. The system resembles a hydrogen atom, but with important modifications:

1. **Dielectric screening**: The Coulomb interaction is screened by the dielectric constant of the semiconductor
2. **Effective mass**: The electron moves with effective mass rather than free electron mass

**Derivation of Impurity Binding Energy**

The Schr├Čdinger equation for the donor electron is identical to the hydrogen atom problem with substitutions: electron mass becomes effective mass, and charge is screened by dielectric constant.

The donor binding energy is:

$$
E_D = -\frac{m^*}{m_0} \cdot \frac{1}{\varepsilon^2} \cdot 13.6 \text{ eV}
$$

For typical semiconductors with effective mass ratio of 0.1 and dielectric constant around 10, the binding energy is approximately 10-50 meV, much smaller than typical band gaps.

**Bohr Radius of Impurity States**

The effective Bohr radius is much larger than the lattice spacing, justifying the use of the effective mass approximation and dielectric screening.

## Part II: Statistical Mechanics of Carriers

### 2.1 The Maxwell-Boltzmann Approximation

**Validity Condition**

In metals, electrons are degenerate with Fermi energy much greater than thermal energy. In semiconductors, the Fermi level lies in the gap, far from band edges.

The Fermi-Dirac distribution when E - E_F >> k_B T becomes approximately:

$$
f(E) \approx e^{-(E-E_F)/k_B T}
$$

This is the **Maxwell-Boltzmann distribution**. Semiconductor carriers behave like a classical ideal gas, not a degenerate Fermi gas.

**Physical Interpretation**

The occupation probability is small, so Pauli exclusion principle is effectively irrelevant. This is the opposite limit from metals and greatly simplifies statistical mechanics.

### 2.2 Electron and Hole Concentrations

**Density of States in Conduction Band**

Near the conduction band minimum with parabolic dispersion, the density of states is:

$$
N_C(E) = \frac{(2m_e^*)^{3/2}}{2\pi^2\hbar^3} \sqrt{E - E_C}
$$

where the effective mass is the electron effective mass.

**Electron Concentration Derivation**

The total electron concentration in the conduction band is:

$$
n = \int_{E_C}^{\infty} N_C(E) f(E) dE
$$

Substituting the Maxwell-Boltzmann approximation:

$$
n = \frac{(2m_e^*)^{3/2}}{2\pi^2\hbar^3} \int_{E_C}^{\infty} \sqrt{E - E_C} \cdot e^{-(E-E_F)/k_B T} dE
$$

Let u = E - E_C, then E - E_F = u + E_C - E_F:

$$
n = \frac{(2m_e^*)^{3/2}}{2\pi^2\hbar^3} e^{-(E_C-E_F)/k_B T} \int_0^{\infty} \sqrt{u} \cdot e^{-u/k_B T} du
$$

The integral is a standard Gamma function:

$$
\int_0^{\infty} u^{1/2} e^{-u/k_B T} du = (k_B T)^{3/2} \Gamma(3/2) = (k_B T)^{3/2} \frac{\sqrt{\pi}}{2}
$$

Therefore:

$$
n = \frac{(2m_e^*)^{3/2}}{2\pi^2\hbar^3} e^{-(E_C-E_F)/k_B T} \cdot (k_B T)^{3/2} \frac{\sqrt{\pi}}{2}
$$

Simplifying:

$$
n = 2\left(\frac{m_e^* k_B T}{2\pi\hbar^2}\right)^{3/2} e^{-(E_C-E_F)/k_B T}
$$

**Effective Density of States**

Define the effective density of states in the conduction band:

$$
N_C = 2\left(\frac{m_e^* k_B T}{2\pi\hbar^2}\right)^{3/2}
$$

Then the electron concentration becomes simply:

$$
n = N_C e^{-(E_C-E_F)/k_B T}
$$

This has a simple interpretation: the electron concentration equals the effective density of states at the conduction band edge, multiplied by the Boltzmann factor for the energy difference.

**Hole Concentration**

Similarly for holes in the valence band, the effective density of states is:

$$
N_V = 2\left(\frac{m_h^* k_B T}{2\pi\hbar^2}\right)^{3/2}
$$

And the hole concentration:

$$
p = N_V e^{-(E_F-E_V)/k_B T}
$$

Note that for holes, the energy difference is E_F - E_V because holes have higher energy when they are lower in the band diagram.

### 2.3 The Mass Action Law

**Product of Carrier Concentrations**

Multiply the expressions for n and p:

$$
np = N_C N_V e^{-(E_C-E_F)/k_B T} \cdot e^{-(E_F-E_V)/k_B T} = N_C N_V e^{-(E_C-E_V)/k_B T}
$$

The Fermi level cancels out! Defining the band gap E_g = E_C - E_V:

$$
np = N_C N_V e^{-E_g/k_B T}
$$

This is the **law of mass action** for semiconductors. It states that the product of electron and hole concentrations is constant at a given temperature, independent of doping.

**Intrinsic Carrier Concentration**

For an intrinsic (pure) semiconductor, n = p = n_i (intrinsic carrier concentration). From the mass action law:

$$
n_i = \sqrt{N_C N_V} e^{-E_g/2k_B T}
$$

The intrinsic carrier concentration depends exponentially on the band gap divided by twice the thermal energy. This strong temperature dependence is characteristic of semiconductor behavior.

**Position of Fermi Level in Intrinsic Semiconductor**

Setting n = p in the intrinsic case:

$$
N_C e^{-(E_C-E_i)/k_B T} = N_V e^{-(E_i-E_V)/k_B T}
$$

Taking logarithms and solving for E_i (intrinsic Fermi level):

$$
E_i = \frac{E_C + E_V}{2} + \frac{3}{4}k_B T \ln\left(\frac{m_h^*}{m_e^*}\right)
$$

If the effective masses are equal, the intrinsic Fermi level lies exactly at mid-gap. In general, it is shifted slightly toward the band with lighter effective mass.

### 2.4 Doped Semiconductors: Charge Neutrality

**Donor Ionization**

Consider an n-type semiconductor with donor concentration N_D. At finite temperature, donors can be ionized:

$$
N_D^+ = N_D \left[1 - f(E_D)\right] = \frac{N_D}{1 + e^{-(E_D-E_F)/k_B T}}
$$

At room temperature, k_B T is comparable to or larger than the donor binding energy, so most donors are ionized.

**Charge Neutrality Condition**

The total positive charge must equal the total negative charge:

$$
n + N_A^- = p + N_D^+
$$

where N_A^- is the ionized acceptor concentration.

For an n-type semiconductor with N_D donors and negligible acceptors:

$$
n = p + N_D^+
$$

At moderate temperatures where n >> p (most electrons come from donors, not thermal excitation across gap):

$$
n \approx N_D^+ \approx N_D
$$

The Fermi level is then:

$$
N_C e^{-(E_C-E_F)/k_B T} = N_D
$$

Solving for E_F:

$$
E_F = E_C - k_B T \ln\left(\frac{N_C}{N_D}\right)
$$

Since N_C is typically 10^19 cm^-3 and N_D is 10^15-10^17 cm^-3, the Fermi level lies below E_C by several k_B T.

## Part III: Direct and Indirect Band Gaps

### 3.1 Optical Absorption and Band Structure

**Momentum Conservation in Optical Transitions**

When a photon is absorbed, exciting an electron from the valence band to the conduction band, both energy and momentum must be conserved.

Photon energy: E_photon = h╬Į
Photon momentum: p_photon = h/╬╗ = E_photon/c

For a photon with energy equal to a typical band gap (1 eV):

$$
p_{photon} = \frac{1 \text{ eV}}{c} \approx 5 \times 10^{-28} \text{ kg m/s} = \frac{\hbar}{400 \text{ nm}}
$$

Compare this to the typical electron momentum at the Brillouin zone boundary:

$$
p_{BZ} \sim \frac{\hbar}{a} \sim \frac{\hbar}{0.5 \text{ nm}}
$$

The photon momentum is negligible compared to the Brillouin zone scale. Therefore, optical transitions are essentially **vertical** in k-space: the electron wavevector does not change.

**Direct Band Gap Semiconductors**

In a direct gap semiconductor (GaAs, InP), the conduction band minimum and valence band maximum occur at the same k-point (usually k=0, the Gamma point).

The absorption coefficient for direct transitions is:

$$
\alpha(h\nu) \propto \sqrt{h\nu - E_g}
$$

Above the band gap, absorption is strong because momentum conservation is automatically satisfied.

**Indirect Band Gap Semiconductors**

In an indirect gap semiconductor (Si, Ge), the conduction band minimum and valence band maximum occur at different k-points.

For Si:
- Valence band maximum at k=0 (Gamma point)
- Conduction band minimum at k Ōēł 0.85 ├Ś (2ŽĆ/a) along [100] direction

A direct vertical transition from the valence band maximum to the conduction band minimum would violate momentum conservation. The transition requires the assistance of a phonon to provide the necessary momentum change.

**Phonon-Assisted Transitions**

The transition rate for phonon-assisted absorption involves second-order perturbation theory:

1. Electron absorbs photon, making virtual transition to intermediate state
2. Electron absorbs or emits phonon, reaching final state in conduction band

The absorption coefficient is much weaker than for direct gaps:

$$
\alpha_{indirect} \propto (h\nu - E_g \pm \hbar\omega_{phonon})^2
$$

The threshold is shifted by the phonon energy, and the quadratic rather than square-root dependence reflects the phase space for two-particle processes.

**Implications for Devices**

Direct gap semiconductors are essential for:
- LEDs (efficient radiative recombination)
- Laser diodes
- Photodetectors at band edge

Indirect gap semiconductors like Si have:
- Longer minority carrier lifetimes (recombination requires phonon)
- Weaker absorption near band edge
- Dominance of non-radiative recombination pathways

## Part IV: Transport Properties

### 4.1 Conductivity in Semiconductors

**Drift Current**

Under an applied electric field E, electrons and holes acquire drift velocities:

$$
v_{d,n} = -\frac{e\tau_n}{m_e^*} E = -\mu_n E
$$
$$
v_{d,p} = \frac{e\tau_p}{m_h^*} E = \mu_p E
$$

where the mobilities are defined as:

$$
\mu_n = \frac{e\tau_n}{m_e^*}, \quad \mu_p = \frac{e\tau_p}{m_h^*}
$$

The total current density is the sum of electron and hole contributions:

$$
J = J_n + J_p = (-en)v_{d,n} + (ep)v_{d,p} = e(n\mu_n + p\mu_p)E
$$

The conductivity is:

$$
\sigma = e(n\mu_n + p\mu_p)
$$

**Intrinsic vs. Extrinsic Conductivity**

For intrinsic semiconductors where n = p = n_i:

$$
\sigma_i = en_i(\mu_n + \mu_p)
$$

Since n_i increases exponentially with temperature, intrinsic conductivity rises rapidly with T.

For n-type semiconductors where n Ōēł N_D and p = n_i^2/N_D << n:

$$
\sigma_n \approx eN_D\mu_n
$$

At low temperatures, conductivity is nearly constant (dominated by constant donor concentration). At high temperatures, intrinsic carriers dominate and conductivity rises exponentially.

### 4.2 Temperature Dependence of Mobility

**Scattering Mechanisms**

Carrier mobility is limited by various scattering processes:

1. **Acoustic phonon scattering** (dominant at moderate temperatures):
   - Scattering rate proportional to temperature
   - Mobility decreases as T^(-3/2)

2. **Ionized impurity scattering** (dominant at low temperatures):
   - Coulomb scattering from charged donors/acceptors
   - Decreases with increasing temperature (thermal velocity averages over Coulomb potential)
   - Mobility increases with T^(3/2)

3. **Optical phonon scattering** (dominant at high temperatures):
   - Requires phonon energy comparable to k_B T
   - Strong temperature dependence

**Combined Temperature Dependence**

The overall mobility typically shows:
- Decrease at low T (ionized impurity scattering)
- Minimum at intermediate T
- Decrease at high T (phonon scattering)

Matthiessen's rule combines scattering rates:

$$
\frac{1}{\mu} = \frac{1}{\mu_{phonon}} + \frac{1}{\mu_{impurity}}
$$

### 4.3 The Hall Effect in Semiconductors

**Measurement of Carrier Type and Density**

The Hall effect provides direct measurement of carrier properties. With magnetic field B perpendicular to current J, a transverse Hall field develops.

For electrons with velocity v_x in electric field E_x and magnetic field B_z:

$$
F_y = -e(E_y - v_x B_z) = 0 \quad \text{(in steady state)}
$$

So:

$$
E_y = v_x B_z = -\frac{J_x}{ne} B_z
$$

The Hall coefficient is:

$$
R_H = \frac{E_y}{J_x B_z} = -\frac{1}{ne}
$$

For p-type semiconductors with hole current:

$$
R_H = +\frac{1}{pe}
$$

The sign of the Hall coefficient directly identifies the carrier type.

**Two-Band Model**

When both electrons and holes contribute:

$$
R_H = \frac{p\mu_p^2 - n\mu_n^2}{e(p\mu_p + n\mu_n)^2}
$$

For intrinsic material (n = p = n_i), if ╬╝_n > ╬╝_p, the Hall coefficient is negative even though carrier concentrations are equal.

**Applications**

Hall measurements determine:
- Carrier type (n or p)
- Carrier concentration
- Mobility (combined with conductivity measurement)
- Temperature dependence of these parameters

## Part V: The p-n Junction

### 5.1 Formation of the Junction

**Bringing n-type and p-type Together**

When n-type and p-type semiconductors are brought into contact:
1. Electrons diffuse from n-side (high concentration) to p-side
2. Holes diffuse from p-side to n-side
3. This leaves behind charged ionized donors (positive) on n-side and acceptors (negative) on p-side
4. The resulting electric field opposes further diffusion
5. Equilibrium is reached when drift current balances diffusion current

**The Depletion Region**

Near the junction, mobile carriers are depleted, leaving a region of space charge:
- n-side: positive space charge from ionized donors N_D+
- p-side: negative space charge from ionized acceptors N_A-

The width of the depletion region W depends on doping levels and temperature.

### 5.2 Built-in Potential

**Equilibrium Condition**

At equilibrium, the chemical potential (Fermi level) must be constant throughout the device. The Fermi level positions in the bulk n and p regions are:

$$
E_{F,n} = E_C - k_B T \ln\left(\frac{N_C}{N_D}\right)
$$
$$
E_{F,p} = E_V + k_B T \ln\left(\frac{N_V}{N_A}\right)
$$

The built-in potential eV_bi is the energy difference between the conduction band edges:

$$
eV_{bi} = E_{C,n} - E_{C,p} = E_{F,n} - E_{F,p} = k_B T \ln\left(\frac{N_D N_A}{n_i^2}\right)
$$

Typical values are 0.5-0.8 V for Si junctions.

**Band Bending**

The electrostatic potential varies across the junction, causing the energy bands to bend:
- Conduction band bends down from n-side to p-side
- Valence band follows the same curvature
- The total bending equals eV_bi

### 5.3 The Depletion Approximation

**Step Junction Model**

Assume abrupt transition from n-type to p-type at x=0. The charge density in the depletion region is:

- n-side (0 < x < x_n): Žü = +eN_D
- p-side (-x_p < x < 0): Žü = -eN_A

**Solving Poisson's Equation**

Poisson's equation relates potential to charge density:

$$
\frac{d^2\phi}{dx^2} = -\frac{\rho}{\varepsilon}
$$

On the n-side:

$$
\frac{d^2\phi}{dx^2} = -\frac{eN_D}{\varepsilon}
$$

Integrating once:

$$
\frac{d\phi}{dx} = -\frac{eN_D}{\varepsilon}x + C_1
$$

Boundary condition: electric field is zero at x = x_n (edge of depletion region), so:

$$
\frac{d\phi}{dx} = \frac{eN_D}{\varepsilon}(x_n - x)
$$

Integrating again:

$$
\phi(x) = \phi_n - \frac{eN_D}{2\varepsilon}(x_n - x)^2
$$

Similarly for the p-side:

$$
\phi(x) = \phi_p + \frac{eN_A}{2\varepsilon}(x_p + x)^2
$$

**Matching Conditions**

At x = 0, potential and electric field must be continuous:

$$
\phi_n - \frac{eN_D}{2\varepsilon}x_n^2 = \phi_p + \frac{eN_A}{2\varepsilon}x_p^2
$$
$$
eN_D x_n = eN_A x_p
$$

The second equation expresses charge neutrality: total positive charge equals total negative charge.

**Depletion Widths**

From charge neutrality:

$$
x_n = \frac{N_A}{N_D + N_A}W, \quad x_p = \frac{N_D}{N_D + N_A}W
$$

where W = x_n + x_p is the total depletion width.

The potential difference is:

$$
V_{bi} = \frac{e}{2\varepsilon}(N_D x_n^2 + N_A x_p^2) = \frac{e}{2\varepsilon}\frac{N_D N_A}{N_D + N_A}W^2
$$

Solving for W:

$$
W = \sqrt{\frac{2\varepsilon V_{bi}}{e}\left(\frac{1}{N_D} + \frac{1}{N_A}\right)}
$$

For asymmetric junctions where N_D >> N_A:

$$
W \approx \sqrt{\frac{2\varepsilon V_{bi}}{eN_A}}
$$

The depletion region extends primarily into the lightly doped side.

### 5.4 Current-Voltage Characteristics

**The Diode Equation**

Under applied bias V:
- Forward bias (p positive relative to n): barrier reduced to e(V_bi - V)
- Reverse bias: barrier increased to e(V_bi + |V|)

The current-voltage relationship is:

$$
J = J_0\left(e^{eV/k_B T} - 1\right)
$$

This is the Shockley diode equation.

**Derivation of Saturation Current**

The saturation current J_0 is the sum of minority carrier diffusion currents:

$$
J_0 = e\left(\frac{D_n n_{p0}}{L_n} + \frac{D_p p_{n0}}{L_p}\right)
$$

where:
- n_p0 = n_i┬▓/N_A is equilibrium minority electron concentration on p-side
- p_n0 = n_i┬▓/N_D is equilibrium minority hole concentration on n-side
- D_n, D_p are diffusion coefficients
- L_n, L_p are diffusion lengths

Using the Einstein relation D = (k_B T/e)╬╝:

$$
J_0 = ek_B T\left(\frac{\mu_n n_{p0}}{L_n} + \frac{\mu_p p_{n0}}{L_p}\right)
$$

**Forward Bias Characteristics**

For V >> k_B T/e:

$$
J \approx J_0 e^{eV/k_B T}
$$

The current increases exponentially with voltage. A small voltage increase of 60 mV (at room temperature) increases current by a factor of e^2.3 Ōēł 10.

**Reverse Bias Characteristics**

For V < 0 with |V| >> k_B T/e:

$$
J \approx -J_0
$$

The current saturates at -J_0, a small value determined by minority carrier concentrations. In reality, generation currents in the depletion region cause the reverse current to be larger and weakly voltage-dependent.

### 5.5 Junction Capacitance

**Depletion Capacitance**

The depletion region acts as a parallel plate capacitor with capacitance:

$$
C_j = \frac{\varepsilon A}{W}
$$

Substituting the expression for W:

$$
C_j = A\sqrt{\frac{e\varepsilon N_A N_D}{2(N_A + N_D)(V_{bi} - V)}}
$$

For an asymmetric junction (N_D >> N_A):

$$
C_j \approx A\sqrt{\frac{e\varepsilon N_A}{2(V_{bi} - V)}}
$$

The capacitance varies as (V_bi - V)^(-1/2) for abrupt junctions. This voltage dependence is utilized in varactor diodes for tuning circuits.
