# Semiconductor Electron Theory: From Band Structure to p-n Junctions

📅 **Date:** 2026-04-02 | 🏷️ **Tags:** Solid State Physics, Semiconductors, p-n Junctions | 📂 **Category:** Condensed Matter Notes

---

## Introduction

Semiconductors occupy a unique position in condensed matter physics, bridging the gap between insulating and metallic behavior. Unlike metals where the Fermi level lies within a partially filled band, semiconductors have a small band gap separating a filled valence band from an empty conduction band.

---

## Part I: Band Structure and Impurity Levels

### 1.1 The Fundamental Difference: Metals vs. Semiconductors

**The Band Picture**

In the single-electron band theory, the key distinction between materials arises from the position of the Fermi energy relative to the band structure:

- **Metals**: Fermi level lies within a band - partially filled states allow conduction
- **Insulators**: Fermi level lies in a large band gap (> 5 eV) - no thermal excitation across gap at room temperature  
- **Semiconductors**: Fermi level lies in a small gap (0.1-3 eV) - measurable thermal excitation at room temperature

**The Role of Temperature**

For a band gap of 1 eV at room temperature:

$$
\\frac{E_g}{k_B T} = \\frac{1 \\text{ eV}}{0.025 \\text{ eV}} \\approx 40
$$

While this ratio is large, the Boltzmann factor gives a small but not negligible occupation when multiplied by the enormous density of states.

### 1.2 Intrinsic Semiconductors: Carrier Generation

**Thermal Excitation Across the Gap**

In a pure (intrinsic) semiconductor, charge carriers are generated by thermal excitation of electrons from the valence band to the conduction band. Each such excitation creates:
- One electron in the conduction band (negative charge)
- One hole in the valence band (positive charge)

This electron-hole pair is the fundamental excitation in semiconductor physics.

### 1.3 Extrinsic Semiconductors: Doping

**The Concept of Doping**

The controlled introduction of impurities (doping) allows us to engineer the carrier concentration and type:

- **n-type**: Addition of donor impurities provides extra electrons
- **p-type**: Addition of acceptor impurities provides extra holes

**Hydrogenic Impurity Model**

Consider a donor atom with one extra valence electron. The donor binding energy is:

$$
E_D = -\\frac{m^*}{m_0} \\cdot \\frac{1}{\\varepsilon^2} \\cdot 13.6 \\text{ eV}
$$

For typical semiconductors with effective mass ratio of 0.1 and dielectric constant around 10, the binding energy is approximately 10-50 meV.

## Part II: Statistical Mechanics of Carriers

### 2.1 The Maxwell-Boltzmann Approximation

**Validity Condition**

In metals, electrons are degenerate with Fermi energy much greater than thermal energy. In semiconductors, the Fermi level lies in the gap, far from band edges.

The Fermi-Dirac distribution when E - E_F >> k_B T becomes approximately:

$$
f(E) \\approx e^{-(E-E_F)/k_B T}
$$

This is the **Maxwell-Boltzmann distribution**. Semiconductor carriers behave like a classical ideal gas, not a degenerate Fermi gas.

**Physical Interpretation**

The occupation probability is small, so Pauli exclusion principle is effectively irrelevant. This is the opposite limit from metals and greatly simplifies statistical mechanics.

### 2.2 Electron and Hole Concentrations

**Effective Density of States**

Define the effective density of states in the conduction band:

$$
N_C = 2\\left(\\frac{m_e^* k_B T}{2\\pi\\hbar^2}\\right)^{3/2}
$$

Then the electron concentration:

$$
n = N_C e^{-(E_C-E_F)/k_B T}
$$

Similarly for holes:

$$
p = N_V e^{-(E_F-E_V)/k_B T}
$$

where:

$$
N_V = 2\\left(\\frac{m_h^* k_B T}{2\\pi\\hbar^2}\\right)^{3/2}
$$

### 2.3 The Mass Action Law

Multiply n and p:

$$
np = N_C N_V e^{-(E_C-E_V)/k_B T} = N_C N_V e^{-E_g/k_B T}
$$

This is constant at given temperature, independent of doping.

**Intrinsic Carrier Concentration**

For intrinsic semiconductors where n = p = n_i:

$$
n_i = \\sqrt{N_C N_V} e^{-E_g/2k_B T}
$$

The intrinsic carrier concentration depends exponentially on band gap divided by twice thermal energy.

### 2.4 Doped Semiconductors

For n-type with donor concentration N_D:

Charge neutrality gives approximately n ≈ N_D at moderate temperatures.

The Fermi level position:

$$
E_F = E_C - k_B T \\ln\\left(\\frac{N_C}{N_D}\\right)
$$

Since N_C ~ 10^19 cm^-3 and N_D ~ 10^15-10^17 cm^-3, the Fermi level lies below E_C by several k_B T.

## Part III: Direct and Indirect Band Gaps

### 3.1 Optical Absorption

**Momentum Conservation**

Photon momentum is negligible compared to Brillouin zone scale. Therefore, optical transitions are **vertical** in k-space.

**Direct Band Gap**

Conduction band minimum and valence band maximum at same k-point (e.g., GaAs, InP). Absorption coefficient:

$$
\\alpha(h\\nu) \\propto \\sqrt{h\\nu - E_g}
$$

**Indirect Band Gap**

Conduction band minimum and valence band maximum at different k-points (e.g., Si, Ge). Requires phonon assistance. Much weaker absorption.

## Part IV: Transport Properties

### 4.1 Conductivity

Drift current under electric field E:

$$
J = e(n\\mu_n + p\\mu_p)E
$$

Conductivity:

$$
\\sigma = e(n\\mu_n + p\\mu_p)
$$

where mobilities are:

$$
\\mu_n = \\frac{e\\tau_n}{m_e^*}, \\quad \\mu_p = \\frac{e\\tau_p}{m_h^*}
$$

### 4.2 Temperature Dependence of Mobility

Scattering mechanisms:
1. Acoustic phonon scattering: mobility ~ T^(-3/2)
2. Ionized impurity scattering: mobility ~ T^(3/2)
3. Optical phonon scattering at high T

Matthiessen's rule:

$$
\\frac{1}{\\mu} = \\frac{1}{\\mu_{phonon}} + \\frac{1}{\\mu_{impurity}}
$$

### 4.3 Hall Effect

Hall coefficient:

For electrons: R_H = -1/(ne)
For holes: R_H = +1/(pe)

The sign identifies carrier type.

## Part V: The p-n Junction

### 5.1 Formation of Junction

When n-type and p-type semiconductors contact:
1. Electrons diffuse from n to p
2. Holes diffuse from p to n
3. Leaves ionized donors (+) on n-side, acceptors (-) on p-side
4. Electric field opposes further diffusion
5. Equilibrium when drift balances diffusion

### 5.2 Built-in Potential

Built-in voltage:

$$
eV_{bi} = k_B T \\ln\\left(\\frac{N_D N_A}{n_i^2}\\right)
$$

Typical values: 0.5-0.8 V for Si.

### 5.3 Depletion Region

Depletion width:

$$
W = \\sqrt{\\frac{2\\varepsilon V_{bi}}{e}\\left(\\frac{1}{N_D} + \\frac{1}{N_A}\\right)}
$$

For asymmetric junction (N_D >> N_A):

$$
W \\approx \\sqrt{\\frac{2\\varepsilon V_{bi}}{eN_A}}
$$

### 5.4 Current-Voltage Characteristics

Shockley diode equation:

$$
J = J_0\\left(e^{eV/k_B T} - 1\\right)
$$

Saturation current:

$$
J_0 = e\\left(\\frac{D_n n_{p0}}{L_n} + \\frac{D_p p_{n0}}{L_p}\\right)
$$

**Forward bias**: Exponential current increase
**Reverse bias**: Small saturation current

### 5.5 Junction Capacitance

Depletion capacitance:

$$
C_j = \\frac{\\varepsilon A}{W} = A\\sqrt{\\frac{e\\varepsilon N_A N_D}{2(N_A + N_D)(V_{bi} - V)}}
$$

Varies as (V_bi - V)^(-1/2) for abrupt junctions.

## Summary

| Parameter | Formula | Notes |
|-----------|---------|-------|
| Intrinsic carrier concentration | n_i = sqrt(N_C N_V) exp(-E_g/2k_B T) | Strong T dependence |
| Electron concentration | n = N_C exp(-(E_C-E_F)/k_B T) | Maxwell-Boltzmann statistics |
| Hole concentration | p = N_V exp(-(E_F-E_V)/k_B T) | Valid when E_F in gap |
| Mass action law | np = n_i^2 | Independent of doping |
| Conductivity | σ = e(nμ_n + pμ_p) | Sum of electron and hole contributions |
| Built-in potential | V_bi = (k_B T/e) ln(N_D N_A/n_i^2) | Across p-n junction |
| Diode current | J = J_0(exp(eV/k_B T) - 1) | Ideal diode equation |

Semiconductors exemplify the power of statistical mechanics in determining macroscopic properties. The key distinction from metals is the applicability of classical Maxwell-Boltzmann statistics due to low carrier concentrations, enabling the rich variety of phenomena that underlie modern electronics.
